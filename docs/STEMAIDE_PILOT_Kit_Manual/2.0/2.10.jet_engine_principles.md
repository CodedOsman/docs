**Student & Teacher Manual**

**1. Project Overview**

The jet engine is the most important propulsion innovation in aviation history. Understanding how it converts fuel energy into thrust — through the Brayton cycle of compression, combustion, and expansion — is foundational to all aviation engineering knowledge. This project combines a classroom-based thermodynamic analysis of the Brayton cycle with a hands-on water pressure rocket build and launch, giving students direct physical experience of Newton's Third Law thrust and the effect of propulsion variables on flight performance.

**Learning Objectives**

* Label and explain all 5 stages of the Brayton thermodynamic cycle as applied to a gas turbine engine
* Calculate thrust output from a simplified jet engine model given mass flow rate, exhaust velocity, and intake velocity
* Explain how a water rocket demonstrates Newton's Third Law in the same way as a jet engine nozzle
* Build a water pressure rocket from a 1.5-litre plastic bottle with foam nose cone and four fins
* Test at least 3 variable configurations and identify the combination that achieves maximum altitude
* Explain the physics of why too much water or too little water reduces rocket performance

**3. Build Steps & Assembly**

**Lesson 1 – Brayton Cycle Theory**

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 1** | **Brayton Cycle Diagram** |
|  | * Students draw a labelled cross-section of a turbojet engine showing: inlet, compressor stages, combustion chamber, turbine stages, and exhaust nozzle * Below the diagram, draw a temperature-entropy (T-s) graph showing temperature at each stage * Label: Stage 1 (Intake) → Stage 2 (Compression) → Stage 3 (Combustion) → Stage 4 (Expansion) → Stage 5 (Exhaust) * Refer to the Brayton Cycle Table in Section 5 for temperature and pressure changes at each stage |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 2** | **Thrust Calculation Worksheet** |
|  | * Thrust formula: F = ṁ × (Ve – Vi) * Where: ṁ = mass flow rate (kg/s), Ve = exhaust velocity (m/s), Vi = intake velocity (m/s) * Given for a small turbofan: ṁ = 50 kg/s, Ve = 400 m/s, Vi = 200 m/s * Calculate: F = 50 × (400 – 200) = 10,000 N = 10 kN thrust * Discussion: a Boeing 737-800 has two CFM56 engines, each producing ~100 kN. How does this compare to your calculation? |

**Lesson 2 – Rocket Build**

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 3** | **Nose Cone Construction** |
|  | * Cut a circle of foam board (10 cm radius); cut a straight line from centre to edge (creating a sector) * Curl into a cone shape; glue the seam; allow to cure * Press 50 g of clay inside the nose cone tip as nose weight — this moves the CG forward of the CP * Tape the nose cone firmly to the top of the bottle |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 4** | **Fin Construction & Attachment** |
|  | * Cut four identical right-triangle fins from foam board: base 6 cm, height 10 cm * Mark four equally-spaced positions on the bottle (90° apart) * Glue fins to the bottle at the marked positions; tape over each fin root * Check symmetry: sight along the bottle from the nose; all four fins should be equally spaced |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 5** | **Launch Rig Setup** |
|  | * Push the launch rod vertically through the launch base; confirm it is vertical using a set square * Slide the rocket neck-first over the launch rod (rod guides the rocket for the first 40 cm) * Add water: 30% fill for first launch (pour in, then insert rubber stopper firmly) * Connect bicycle pump needle adapter through the rubber stopper; pump to 80 PSI |

**Lessons 3–4 – Launches & Data Collection**

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 6** | **Launch Protocol** |
|  | * All students stand at least 5 m behind the launch rig; launch operator operates pump only * Teacher releases the rocket by pulling a release cord or sliding the guide * Observe the flight; estimate maximum height; measure horizontal distance with a tape measure * Record all data immediately in the rocket data table * Change one variable for each subsequent launch (water level, angle, or fin size) |

**4. Power & Safety Notes**

|  |
| --- |
| **⚠ Safety Requirements**  All observers must stand at least 5 m behind the rocket before launch.  Maximum pump pressure: 80 PSI. Never exceed this — the bottle can rupture above 100 PSI.  Eye protection (goggles) is mandatory for the launch operator and all observers within 10 m.  Wet grass: the launch area will be soaked with water after each launch — watch for slipping.  Do not launch toward buildings, roads, or overhead power lines.  Never put your face near the bottle neck while pumping — the stopper can eject suddenly. |

**5. Engineering Principles**

**The Brayton Thermodynamic Cycle**

| **Stage** | **What Happens** | **Temperature Change** | **Pressure Change** | **Component** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1 – Intake** | Air drawn into engine | Slight increase | Slight increase | Inlet duct |
| **2 – Compression** | Air compressed mechanically | Large increase (+300°C) | Large increase (×30) | Compressor |
| **3 – Combustion** | Fuel added; air-fuel mix ignited | Very large increase (+1000°C) | Constant (approx.) | Combustion chamber |
| **4 – Expansion** | Hot gas expands through turbine | Decreases (work extracted) | Drops | Turbine |
| **5 – Exhaust** | Gas expelled at high speed | Further decrease | Returns to ambient | Nozzle |

|  |
| --- |
| **Newton's Third Law – Rocket & Jet Engine**  Jet engine: air is accelerated rearward at high speed (action). The engine — and aircraft — is pushed forward (reaction).  Water rocket: water is expelled downward at high speed (action). The rocket is pushed upward (reaction).  Both systems demonstrate the same physics: F = ṁ × ΔV (thrust = mass flow rate × velocity change).  The key difference: a jet engine operates continuously; a water rocket exhausts all propellant in the first 0.5 seconds. |

**8. Common Errors & Fixes**

| **Error** | **Likely Cause** | **Fix** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Rocket will not launch** | Pump pressure too low or seal leaking | Increase pump strokes to 80+; check rubber stopper seal; ensure stopper fits tightly |
| **Rocket tumbles in flight** | Fins asymmetric or CG behind CP | Check fin alignment; add clay to nose to move CG forward |
| **Very short flight** | Too little water (< 25%) | Try 30–40% water fill; this range usually gives best performance |
| **Water sprays sideways** | Stopper not centred in bottle neck | Re-centre stopper; ensure launch rod guides the bottle vertically |
| **Fins fall off during flight** | Insufficient glue on fin roots | Apply glue generously; allow full cure time; tape over the root junction |

**9. Upgrade & Extension Ideas**

* Multi-Stage Rocket: connect two bottles with a coupling; stage-separation mechanism using a rubber band release
* Payload Camera: tape a small action camera to the rocket; record the launch video; calculate height from video frame rate
* Pressure Gauge: add a pressure gauge to the launch rig; record exact launch pressure and compare to altitude
* Turbofan vs. Turbojet: extend the worksheet to compare bypass ratio effects on thrust and fuel efficiency

**10. Teacher Notes & Differentiation**

* The Brayton cycle theory (Lesson 1) is best done with a cut-away jet engine model or video — show the CFM56 engine assembly video (freely available online) before the diagram exercise
* For the rocket launch, mark a 5 m safety radius on the ground with chalk before students arrive at the launch area
* Support – Brayton diagram only (no calculation); pre-built rocket; 1 launch with standard configuration
* Core – Full Brayton cycle with calculation; rocket build; 3 variable launches; data table
* Extension – Multi-stage rocket; payload camera; pressure gauge data
