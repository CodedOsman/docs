**Student & Teacher Manual**

**1. Project Overview**

Rockets are the only propulsion system capable of operating in the vacuum of space — and the physics that launches a water bottle rocket 30 metres into the air is identical to the physics that launches a satellite into orbit. This project builds on the jet engine theory from Project 37, adding a structured experimental investigation of how water fill level, launch angle, fin area, and pressure affect flight performance. Students apply Newton's Third Law in its purest form and experience directly why rocket engineers spend thousands of hours optimising these variables.

**Learning Objectives**

* Apply Newton's Third Law to explain how water expulsion creates rocket thrust
* Explain why an optimal water fill level exists (too little = low mass flow; too much = insufficient compressed air volume)
* Systematically vary 4 rocket parameters: water fill, launch angle, fin area, and pressure
* Record 9 variable launch configurations and identify the combination producing maximum altitude
* Graph at least one variable against performance (height or distance) and identify the trend
* Compare rocket propulsion to jet engine propulsion: one similarity and one key difference

**5. Engineering Principles**

**Rocket Physics**

* Newton's Third Law: water is expelled downward at high velocity (action); rocket is pushed upward (reaction)
* Thrust: F = ṁ × v\_exit. Thrust depends on mass flow rate (how much water per second) and exit velocity (how fast)
* Optimal fill: too little water → low mass flow rate → low thrust. Too much water → little compressed air volume → short thrust duration and low pressure differential
* Fins: act as a pendulum moment — drag on fins (which are behind the CG) stabilises the rocket and prevents spin
* Pressure: higher pressure → higher exit velocity → higher thrust → more height. But diminishing returns above 80 PSI as the rubber seal limits flow

|  |
| --- |
| **Rocket vs Jet Engine – Key Comparison**  SIMILARITY: Both generate thrust by Newton's Third Law — expelling mass rearward at high velocity.  DIFFERENCE 1: A jet engine uses atmospheric oxygen for combustion. A rocket carries its own oxidiser, so it can operate in space where there is no atmosphere.  DIFFERENCE 2: A jet engine operates continuously at steady state. A water rocket exhausts all propellant in 0.3–0.5 seconds, then becomes an unpowered projectile.  REAL WORLD: The Ariane 5 rocket that launched Ghana's first satellite (GhanaSat-1 in 2017) used the same action-reaction principle as this water rocket. |

**8. Common Errors & Fixes**

| **Error** | **Likely Cause** | **Fix** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Rocket barely leaves the rod** | Pressure too low or seal leaking | Pump to 80 PSI; press stopper firmly; replace stopper if seal is poor |
| **Rocket spins in flight** | Fins not symmetrical; one fin larger | Check all four fins are identical; re-tape any that are loose or angled |
| **Very low height with 50% water** | Too much water; not enough air volume | Optimal fill is 25–35%; water above this reduces the compressed air volume |
| **Rocket drifts downwind** | No correction for wind | Aim slightly into the wind; adjust launch angle toward the wind direction |
| **Data table inconsistent** | Different launchers apply different pressure | Standardise: same launcher every flight; same pump stroke count; use a pressure gauge |

**9. Upgrade & Extension Ideas**

* Two-Stage Rocket: build a second bottle that releases from the first at peak altitude; observe altitude improvement
* Altitude Measurement: use a clinometer (angle measured from 10 m away) to calculate exact height using trigonometry
* GhanaSat-1 Research: research Ghana's first satellite; present how it was launched and what it does
* Thrust Curve: attach a spring scale to the launch rod; record the scale reading as the rocket fires — plot the thrust curve over time

**10. Teacher Notes & Differentiation**

* Conduct all outdoor launches in a single session to avoid resetting the safety zone multiple times
* Use a permanent marker to measure bottle volumes: 30% = 450 mL in a 1.5L bottle; 50% = 750 mL — pre-mark these on the bottle before Lesson 2
* The finless launch (Row 7) is the most dramatic demonstration — ensure the safety zone is respected before this attempt
* Support – 5 launches only (water fill variable and angle variable); no graph required; teacher records data
* Core – All 9 variable launches; full data table; graph; written conclusion; rocket vs. jet comparison
* Extension – Two-stage rocket; altitude measurement with clinometer; GhanaSat-1 research; thrust curve
