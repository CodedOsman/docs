**Student & Teacher Manual**

**1. Project Overview**

High-lift devices are among the most important flight control systems on any aircraft. Flaps allow a plane to fly more slowly during approach and landing without stalling, while spoilers allow rapid descent without gaining speed. This project adds functional trailing-edge flaps and upper-surface spoilers to the Project 11 RC aircraft, giving students direct physical experience of how these systems change the aircraft's behaviour — and why every commercial aircraft uses them on every flight.

**Learning Objectives**

* Explain the aerodynamic effect of flap deployment: increased camber, increased lift coefficient, increased drag
* Distinguish between trailing-edge flaps (increase lift) and spoilers (destroy lift / increase drag)
* Design and build a trailing-edge flap mechanism driven by one servo via a twin pushrod linkage
* Design and build an upper-surface spoiler panel driven by a second servo
* Fly five configurations and measure stall speed and landing distance for each
* Explain the pilot procedure for configuring flaps for approach and landing

**3. Build Steps & Assembly**

**Lessons 1–2 – Flap Mechanism**

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 1** | **Design Flap Size** |
|  | * Flap span: each flap covers the inner 40% of each half-wing * Flap chord: 25% of the wing chord (from trailing edge forward) * Score and cut the flap panel from foam board; ensure clean, straight edges * Hinge the flap to the wing trailing edge with a masking tape hinge: tape on the upper surface |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 2** | **Install Flap Servo & Linkage** |
|  | * Mount the flap servo in the fuselage, centred between the two flap panels * Run a pushrod from the servo arm to the left flap control horn * Run a second equal-length pushrod to the right flap control horn * Adjust pushrod lengths until both flaps are neutral (0°) when the servo is centred * Test: assign a 2-position switch (CH5) to flap servo; verify both flaps extend symmetrically to 30° |

**Lessons 3–4 – Spoiler Mechanism**

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 3** | **Design & Install Spoiler Panel** |
|  | * Cut a spoiler panel from foam board: 8 cm × 4 cm per wing (upper surface, centred spanwise) * Hinge the panel at its forward edge (tape hinge on the leading edge of the spoiler panel) * When actuated, the rear of the panel rotates upward 60–90° into the airstream * Mount the spoiler servo in the wing; connect via a short pushrod to the spoiler horn * Assign CH6 to the spoiler servo; verify: switch = extend spoiler panel upward |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 4** | **Configuration Flight Tests** |
|  | * Complete the full pre-flight checklist from Project 11 for every test flight * Fly each of the 5 configurations listed in the Data Table * For stall speed: reduce throttle in level flight until stall onset; note airspeed or estimate from GPS groundspeed * For landing distance: mark the threshold; measure from threshold to full stop * Record all values immediately after each landing |

**4. Power & Safety Notes**

|  |
| --- |
| **⚠ Safety Requirements**  Power: LiPo battery – teacher connects and disconnects only.  First flight with flaps: set flaps to 15° only for the first landing; never deploy full flaps without practising partial first.  Pitch trim: flap deployment will cause a nose-up pitch — set a small down trim before deploying flaps.  Asymmetric flap: if one flap fails to extend, DO NOT fly — asymmetric flap causes severe roll.  Verify both flaps extend equally on the ground before every flight. |

**5. Engineering Principles**

**High-Lift Device Effects**

| **Configuration** | **Flap Angle** | **Effect on Lift** | **Effect on Drag** | **Use Case** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Clean (no flaps)** | 0° | Baseline | Baseline | Cruise; normal flight |
| **Partial flap** | 15° | +20–30% | Moderate increase | Approach; slowing down |
| **Full flap** | 30° | +40–60% | Large increase | Landing; short field |
| **Spoiler deployed** | – | Reduces lift | Large increase | Rapid descent; speed brake |

|  |
| --- |
| **Why Flaps Are Used on Every Landing**  Without flaps, an aircraft must maintain a minimum speed to avoid stalling — this speed determines the minimum runway length needed.  Flaps increase the wing's camber and effective area, generating more lift at lower speeds.  This allows the aircraft to fly the approach at a slower, safer speed without stalling.  A Boeing 737 approaches at approximately 145 knots clean; with full flaps (40°), it can land at 135 knots — saving runway length.  At Kotoka International Airport, every landing aircraft deploys flaps in stages during the approach. |

**8. Common Errors & Fixes**

| **Error** | **Likely Cause** | **Fix** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Flap deflects wrong direction** | Servo arm on wrong side of hinge | Move servo arm to other side; or reverse channel in transmitter |
| **Asymmetric flap extension** | Pushrod lengths unequal on left and right | Trim both pushrods to identical length; verify equal deflection angle |
| **Aircraft pitches nose-up with flaps** | Normal — flaps shift lift rearward | Add slight down elevator trim when flaps are extended |
| **Spoiler causes hard roll** | Spoiler deployed fully on approach — too much drag asymmetry | Deploy spoiler symmetrically on both wings; ensure both panels open equally |
| **Servo stalls (buzzes)** | Hinge friction too high | Sand hinge; add a small amount of petroleum jelly to the hinge line |

**9. Upgrade & Extension Ideas**

* Leading Edge Slats: add a hinged leading-edge panel that drops forward to further increase the wing's effective chord and delay stall
* Flap Position Indicator: add an LED strip on the transmitter that shows flap angle (0°, 15°, 30°) based on servo position
* Short Field Landing Challenge: mark a 10 m landing zone; compete for the shortest full-stop landing distance
* Speed Brake Dive: use the spoiler to arrest a steep dive from altitude; graph altitude vs. time with and without the spoiler

**10. Teacher Notes & Differentiation**

* Teach students to set a small down-elevator trim before deploying flaps — this is standard procedure in real aviation and must become habit before the first flap flight
* For the landing distance comparison, mark the threshold on the grass with two cones; have a second student standing at the landing point to mark where the aircraft stops
* Support – Flaps only (no spoiler); 3 configurations only (clean, 15°, 30°); teacher assists with servo installation
* Core – Both mechanisms; 5 configurations; data table; written conclusion comparing configurations
* Extension – Leading-edge slats; flap position indicator; short-field challenge
