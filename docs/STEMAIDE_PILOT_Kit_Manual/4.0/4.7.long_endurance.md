**Student & Teacher Manual**

**1. Project Overview**

Solar-powered aircraft represent the ultimate goal of sustainable aviation — unlimited endurance powered by the sun. NASA's Helios prototype flew for 17 hours on solar power alone; Airbus's Zephyr S has stayed aloft for 26 days. This project challenges students to build and fly a solar-powered glider capable of sustaining level flight on solar energy alone. Every gram matters, every watt must be accounted for, and the energy balance equation — solar input vs. motor consumption — governs every design decision.

**Learning Objectives**

* Calculate the power budget for the aircraft: solar input (W) vs. motor consumption (W)
* Select a motor and propeller combination optimised for low-speed, high-efficiency cruise
* Install a solar panel array on the wing and connect it to an MPPT charge controller
* Verify the charging system: confirm positive current into the battery in direct sunlight
* Achieve 30+ minutes of sustained powered flight with the battery level stable or rising
* Log the energy balance data every 30 minutes and analyse the power flow

**3. Build Phases & Steps**

**Phase 1: Power Budget & Design (Lessons 1–2)**

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 1** | **Power Budget Calculation** |
|  | * Measure total aircraft all-up weight (AUW) target: < 500 g * Calculate minimum power to sustain level flight: P = (AUW × g × V) / (L/D ratio) * For a typical foam glider: L/D = 10; V = 8 m/s; AUW = 400 g → P ≈ 3.1 W * Solar panel must output more than this in direct noon sun * Select motor: target 80% efficiency at cruise throttle; KV < 900 for 8-inch propeller |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 2** | **Airframe Optimisation** |
|  | * Select the highest-aspect-ratio glider available in Kit 3 — long narrow wings are most efficient * Weigh all components; the solar panel array must not exceed 20% of total AUW * Plan panel mounting locations: upper wing surface; panels must not block control surfaces or add significant drag |

**Phase 2: Build (Lessons 3–6)**

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 3** | **Airframe & Motor Installation** |
|  | * Build or prepare the glider airframe following the Project 11 build steps * Mount the low-KV motor on the nose with a folding propeller * Install servos and control linkages; verify CG at 30% chord from leading edge |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 4** | **Solar Panel Installation** |
|  | * Clean the wing upper surface; lay panels flat — no gaps, no overlap * Connect panels in series to increase voltage (series) or in parallel to increase current (parallel) * Solar panel positive wire → MPPT IN+; negative → MPPT IN– * MPPT OUT+ → battery positive; MPPT OUT– → battery negative * Use a multimeter in bright sun: MPPT OUT should show positive current (charging) |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 5** | **Full System Integration** |
|  | * Connect battery → ESC → motor; confirm motor direction (should push air rearward) * Connect voltage monitor between battery and ESC; confirm it displays battery voltage * Perform a full system test: in direct sun, motor at 50% throttle — battery voltage should hold steady or rise |

**Phase 3: Testing (Lessons 7–10)**

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 6** | **Ground Energy Test** |
|  | * Place aircraft in direct noon sun; measure solar input (W) with watt meter * Run motor at cruise throttle (50%); measure motor power draw * If solar input > motor draw: battery is charging during flight — success * If motor draw > solar input: reduce motor throttle or increase panel area |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 7** | **Glide Test (No Power)** |
|  | * Disconnect battery; launch from a gentle hill or by hand * Confirm the aircraft glides straight and stable for 10+ metres * CG must be correct before any powered flight attempt |

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| **STEP 8** | **Powered Endurance Flight** |
|  | * Connect battery; confirm solar charging active before arming * Fly a continuous circuit; record battery voltage every 10 minutes * Target: 30 minutes with battery voltage stable or rising * Log all energy data in the Energy Balance Table (Section 6) |

**5. Engineering Principles**

**Solar Power System Chain**

| **Component** | **Function** | **Efficiency** | **Key Selection Factor** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Solar panel** | Harvests sunlight → DC electricity | 15–22% | W/g (power per gram of panel weight) |
| **MPPT controller** | Maximises panel power extraction | 90–97% | Match to panel voltage; low quiescent current |
| **Li-ion battery** | Stores excess solar energy | 95–99% (charge/discharge) | Energy density (Wh/g); cycle life |
| **ESC** | Controls motor speed | 95–98% | Current rating ≥ peak motor current |
| **Brushless motor** | Converts electrical energy to rotation | 70–85% | KV: low KV (< 800) for large efficient prop |
| **Propeller** | Converts rotation to thrust | 55–75% | Large diameter, low pitch, slow RPM |

|  |
| --- |
| **The Energy Balance Equation**  For sustained solar flight: P\_solar > P\_motor + P\_avionics  P\_solar (W) = Panel area (m²) × Solar irradiance (W/m²) × Panel efficiency × MPPT efficiency  At noon in Ghana: Solar irradiance ≈ 900–1000 W/m². A 200 cm² panel (0.02 m²) at 20% efficiency = 3.6–4.0 W.  A 400 g aircraft needs approximately 3 W to maintain level flight at 8 m/s.  Therefore, 200 cm² of panel is the theoretical minimum — in practice, aim for 250–300 cm² to allow for clouds and non-ideal angle. |

**Why Ghana is Ideal for Solar Aviation**

* Ghana lies within 10° of the equator — solar irradiance is among the highest in the world (900–1050 W/m² at noon)
* Minimal seasonal variation: solar panels produce consistently near peak output year-round
* This makes Ghana an excellent location for solar UAV operations, agricultural monitoring, and long-endurance surveillance
* The Ghana Meteorological Agency uses solar-powered weather stations — the same solar energy principles in this project

**8. Common Errors & Fixes**

| **Error** | **Likely Cause** | **Fix** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **No solar charge** | Panel wiring error or MPPT not connected | Check polarity; confirm MPPT IN+ goes to panel positive; use multimeter to verify current |
| **Battery depletes during flight** | Motor power exceeds solar input | Reduce throttle; fly in faster thermals; increase panel area; reduce aircraft weight |
| **Aircraft too heavy to fly** | Solar panels overweight | Use flexible thin-film panels; reduce battery capacity; lighten airframe |
| **MPPT gets very hot** | MPPT undersized for panel | Select MPPT rated at 1.5× panel peak current |
| **Motor weak even on full battery** | Motor KV too high for large propeller | Switch to lower KV motor; or use smaller propeller |

**9. Upgrade & Extension Ideas**

* Night Flight Capability – Size the battery to store enough daytime solar energy for 30 minutes of night flight
* Autonomous Soaring – Add a variometer sensor; programme the FC to circle in thermals to gain altitude without using the motor
* Maximum Endurance Challenge – optimise every component; attempt a 2-hour endurance flight
* Solar Tracker – Mount panels on a servo-driven gimbal that tracks the sun angle; compare energy harvest to fixed-panel mounting
* Published Power Budget – Write the energy budget as a formal technical report; compare with published solar UAV data

**10. Teacher Notes & Differentiation**

* Schedule outdoor solar testing for midday (11:00–13:00) when irradiance is maximum — morning or afternoon tests may show insufficient solar output even with a well-designed system
* The power budget calculation (Step 1) is the most important design step — spend a full lesson on it; students who skip it almost always build aircraft that cannot sustain solar flight
* Support – Pre-calculated power budget; teacher selects motor and panel; student focuses on installation, wiring, and energy balance logging
* Core – Full design; power budget; installation; endurance flight; energy log
* Extension – Night capability; autonomous soaring; maximum endurance challenge; solar tracker
